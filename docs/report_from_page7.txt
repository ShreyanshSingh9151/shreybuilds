ABSTRACT
In the modern enterprise, email remains the primary channel for professional communication, yet the
increasing volume of correspondence imposes a significant cognitive load on knowledge workers.
Research indicates that professionals spend approximately 28% of their workweek managing email,
often relying on manual drafting or static, impersonal templates to maintain throughput. This project
addresses the inefficiency and lack of personalization in current communication tools by developing an
Intelligent Automated Email Generator using Java Spring Boot and Large Language Models
(LLMs).
The proposed system bridges the gap between robust enterprise software and state-of-the-art Generative
AI. Unlike Python-based prototypes, this application is architected using Spring Boot, leveraging its
mature ecosystem for concurrency, security, and dependency injection to ensure scalability suitable for
corporate environments. The core innovation lies in the integration of Spring AI, which abstracts the
complexities of interacting with model providers (such as OpenAI) and enables a modular, provideragnostic design.
To overcome the limitations of generic text generation, the system implements Retrieval-Augmented
Generation (RAG). By utilizing a Vector Database (PostgreSQL with pgvector) to store and retrieve
relevant organizational context—such as prior conversation history and project documentation—the
application grounds the LLM’s responses in factual data. This significantly mitigates the risk of
"hallucinations" and ensures that generated drafts are contextually accurate and hyper-personalized. The
methodology further incorporates advanced Prompt Engineering techniques, utilizing structured
templates to dynamically adjust tone and format based on user intent.
The outcome of this project is a resilient, RESTful API capable of synthesizing human-quality email
drafts with low latency. By automating the drafting process while retaining high contextual relevance,
the system demonstrates the potential to reduce email composition time by up to 80%, shifting the user’s
role from writer to reviewer. This report details the architectural decisions, implementation challenges,
and performance evaluations involved in engineering this solution, offering a blueprint for integrating
Generative AI into standard Java enterprise stacks.

Keywords: Generative AI, Java Spring Boot, Large Language Models (LLM), Retrieval-Augmented
Generation (RAG), Spring AI, Automated Communication.

Introduction
In the contemporary digital economy, email remains the undisputed backbone of professional communication, with
global volume reaching approximately 347 billion emails sent daily in 2025. Despite its ubiquity, managing this
influx has become a critical productivity bottleneck. Research indicates that knowledge workers spend roughly 28%
of their workweek reading and responding to emails, often manually drafting repetitive replies that consume valuable
time and mental energy. Traditional automation tools, such as static templates or "mail merge" scripts, fail to solve
this problem effectively; they lack the semantic understanding to generate context-aware, personalized responses,
often resulting in generic communication that disengages recipients.
This project proposes an Intelligent Automated Email Generator to address these challenges by bridging the gap
between enterprise software standards and Generative AI. While Python is the dominant language for AI model
training, Java Spring Boot remains the preferred framework for building scalable, secure, and maintainable enterprise
backends. This system leverages Spring AI, a novel framework that abstracts the complexities of interacting with
Large Language Models (LLMs), enabling seamless integration of AI capabilities into the Java ecosystem without
the need for external Python microservices.
To ensure the generated emails are factually accurate and organizationally relevant, the system implements RetrievalAugmented Generation (RAG). By connecting the LLM to a vector database containing the user's historical data and
context, the application grounds its responses in reality, significantly mitigating the risk of AI "hallucinations" while
delivering hyper-personalized content. This report outlines the architectural design, implementation strategy, and
evaluation of this system, demonstrating a practical approach to engineering intelligent communication tools for the
modern enterprise.
Despite the emergence of various digital communication platforms, email remains a cornerstone of modern
communication. In 2024, the number of email users worldwide had reached 4.48 billion, building on an existing user

base that already encompassed over half the world’s population in 2023 [1]. Furthermore, the volume of email traffic
is enormous. The total number of business and consumer emails sent and received per day reached 361.6 billion in

significantly contributes to the cognitive load of knowledge workers, as 60% of people prefer email for work

Despite its ubiquity, email communication poses persistent challenges that motivate research into automation.
Knowledge workers experience cognitive overload when manag ing high volumes of messages, while organizations
face inefficiencies arising from delayed or inconsistent responses. Traditional rule-based or template-driven
automation lacks personalization and contextual understanding, resulting in impersonal and often irrelevant outputs.
The emergence of Large Language Models (LLMs) offers new possibilities for generating context-aware, stylistically
adaptive, and user-aligned messages. However, these advances also raise critical issues regarding model bias, privacy
protection, and maintaining a consistent tone across multi-turn conversations. Closing these gaps is essential for
achieving effective and trustworthy email automation. This survey examines the transformative role of personalized
LLMs in automating and improving email response generation. Unlike early systems that relied on static templates
or rule-based approaches, contemporary methods increasingly use LLMs to generate contextaware, stylistically
consistent, and personalized responses. However, this shift introduces new challenges, including user privacy
concerns, the risk of bias in automated responses, and difficulty maintaining contextual coherence across email
threads. In light of these challenges, the aim of this review is to summarize the existing approaches and pinpoint open
research questions at the intersection of LLMs and personalized email automation. To address these issues and
improve our understanding of the current situation, we conducted a thorough review of LLM-driven email
personalization. The study focuses on identifying state-of-the-art personalization strategies, technical frameworks,
user perceptions, evaluation methods, and the security implications of deploying such systems. Given the increasing
prevalence and impact of personalized LLMs in digital communication, our research aims to address the following
research questions: RQ1: What are the core strategies and frameworks that enable the effective generation of
8

personalized email responses using LLMs? RQ2: What are the primary technical methods, security vulnerabilities,
user perceptions, and evaluation benchmarks for personalized LLM-based email assistants? This survey is structured
as a systematic literature review, following the PRISMA methodology, to ensure transparency, replicability, and
comprehensive coverage of the research domain. Specifically, we focus on text-based systems powered by LLMs
designed for email response generation. Voice-based assistants, multimodal communication tools, and non-emailcentric dialogue systems are beyond the scope of this survey. Similarly, we exclude works that focus exclusively on
general-purpose personalization techniques that are not applied to email composition or response tasks. Existing
studies on email automation tend to be fragmented, addressing isolated aspects such as response generation or tone
adjustment, rather than integrating personalization, privacy and adaptability. There has been no comprehensive
synthesis connecting the technical methods, ethical implications and user-centric evaluation of LLM-based email
systems. This review aims to address this gap. This work provides the first PRISMA-based survey that focuses
exclusively on personalized LLMs for email automation. It critically compares 32 studies across personalization,
adaptation, and evaluation dimensions, and outlines open challenges—including multimodal, privacy-preserving, and
cross-domain extensions—to guide future research. Therefore, the main contributions of this work are the following:
• A critical analysis of frameworks and tools designed to improve LLM-based writing assistants in email contexts; •
A structured thematic synthesis of the identified research areas and an evaluation of personalization techniques
tailored for email response generation; • An examination of security vulnerabilities associated with personalized email
generation systems;
• A synthesis of user perception studies to understand trust, usability, and satisfaction with AI-mediated
communication;
• An assessment of benchmarking methodologies used to evaluate personalized email systems.

time and reallocate it to core business objectives.
Beyond raw time, manual repetition exacts a heavy toll on cognitive resources. The need to repeatedly ensure brand
compliance, maintain a consistent professional tone, and check for grammatical accuracy across hundreds of similar
messages leads to mental fatigue and burnout. This elevated cognitive load directly contributes to the second major

overly complex or casual one. This variability dilutes brand voice, confuses recipients, and undermines the

Literature Survey
The rapid growth of digital communication has significantly increased the reliance on emails for personal, academic,
and professional interactions. Because writing structured, context-aware, and grammatically accurate emails is often
time-consuming, researchers have focused on automating email generation through artificial intelligence. Early
approaches to email automation used rule-based templates, predefined sentence structures, and heuristic textgeneration methods. While these traditional systems were able to produce simple responses, they lacked flexibility,
contextual understanding, and personalization—leading to repetitive or unnatural outputs.
The emergence of machine learning techniques, particularly natural language processing (NLP), marked an important
shift in text automation. Models based on n-grams, statistical language modeling, and later sequence-to-sequence
(Seq2Seq) frameworks improved fluency and coherence to some extent. However, such systems often struggled with
long-range dependencies, tone adaptation, and dynamic user prompts. The introduction of attention mechanisms,
specifically the Transformer architecture, revolutionized text generation by enabling models to capture rich
contextual relationships across sentences and paragraphs.
Large Language Models (LLMs) such as GPT, BERT-based variants, LLaMA, and other transformer-driven
architectures have become the foundation for modern email automation. These models are pre-trained on massive
text corpora, enabling them to understand semantics, grammar, intent, and communication patterns. Unlike earlier
methods, LLMs can generate complete emails from minimal input, such as keywords or a short prompt, while
adapting tone (formal, informal, persuasive, apologetic), structure (subject, greeting, body, closing), and domainspecific vocabulary.
Recent work in AI-assisted writing has emphasized context awareness, personalization, and optimization. Studies
exploring user-conditioned text generation highlight that LLMs can produce more relevant emails when provided
with additional metadata such as previous email threads, user preferences, or organizational writing guidelines.
Techniques such as fine-tuning, prompt engineering, and retrieval-augmented generation further enhance accuracy
14

by grounding outputs in domain-specific knowledge or real-time information.
Research on automated business communication shows that LLM-based generators significantly reduce writing time
while improving linguistic quality, consistency, and professional tone. Integration of sentiment control, intent
recognition, and summarization enables these models to convert unstructured instructions into coherent email drafts.
Additionally, email classification and reply-suggestion systems have evolved from simple auto-reply features to
intelligent assistants capable of generating personalized responses.
Despite these advances, several challenges remain. Ethical concerns related to data privacy, hallucination, and overreliance on AI-generated communication are widely discussed in recent literature. Studies highlight the need for
human-in-the-loop systems, transparency, and controlled generation to prevent incorrect or harmful outputs. Research
also discusses the computational cost of training and deploying LLM-based applications and explores efficient
alternatives like parameter-efficient fine-tuning and smaller domain-specific LLMs.
Overall, existing literature demonstrates that automatic email generation through LLMs has progressed from rigid
templates to highly adaptive, intelligent text-generation systems capable of producing professional-grade
communication. With continuous advancements in LLM architectures, contextual reasoning, and personalization, AIbased email drafting tools are becoming increasingly practical for real-world applications, from corporate
communication to customer support and academic correspondence. The ongoing research suggests that future systems
will further improve accuracy, reduce human effort, and enable more natural, contextually aligned email interactions.

contextually appropriate, and grammatically correct emails remains time-consuming. Users—from students
and educators to corporate employees and customer service teams—often struggle to convert their thoughts
or raw instructions into polished emails. This leads to delays, inconsistency in communication quality, and
increased cognitive effort.
Although email clients offer features like auto-complete or short smart replies, these solutions are limited.
They cannot generate full-length, purpose-specific emails, nor can they interpret user intent or tone
accurately. Users still spend significant time manually drafting content for tasks such as:
